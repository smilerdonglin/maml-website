Multi-Scale Advanced Manufacturing Laboratory

The Multi-Scale Advanced Manufacturing Laboratory (MAML), led by Professor Dong Lin, advances the science and technology of manufacturing through research spanning additive manufacturing of aerogels, ceramics, metals, and composites; laser processing; and functional nanomaterials. Our work is multidisciplinary, spans from fundamentals through applications — including a Guinness World Record for the least dense 3D-printed structure (0.5 mg/cm³) — and engages collaborators including NIST, national synchrotron facilities, and partner universities worldwide.

We are based in the School of Mechanical, Industrial, and Manufacturing Engineering at Oregon State University. Dr. Lin is a Fellow of ASME and a recipient of the NSF CAREER Award.

Research

Research in MAML connects nanomaterial synthesis, laser–matter interaction, and additive manufacturing process design — from the nanoscale to the machine scale — across eight areas.

Area 1 — 3D Freeze Printing (3DFP) of Aerogels

We invented a freeze-assembly printing route to pure, continuous, boundary-free, and truly three-dimensional aerogels — including overhang structures — from graphene, MXenes, nanocellulose, and silver nanowires. Printed graphene aerogels combine ultra-low density (a Guinness World Record 0.5 mg/cm³), high electrical conductivity (~15.4 S/m), and high compressibility, with in-situ synchrotron X-ray imaging revealing the printing process itself.

Area 2 — Shock Wave–Graphene Interactions

Graphene can be vertically aligned during additive manufacturing (Step 1: laser sintering of graphene oxide/iron nanocomposites). A shock wave introduced by laser shock peening (Step 2) then interacts with the aligned graphene, generating stress concentration and nucleating high densities of nanotwins between graphene sheets under low shock pressure — yielding metal–graphene composites with ultra-stable mechanical properties.

Area 3 — Metal Additive Manufacturing: Laser Powder Bed Fusion & Directed Energy Deposition

Investigating laser scan strategies, beam shaping, and in-situ thermal monitoring to control microstructure and defect formation in LPBF and L-DED. Active projects include defect-free LPBF (Oregon Manufacturing Innovation Center), laser stirring with elliptical scanning for on-demand alloying (in collaboration with NIST), and lattice-structured materials (Idaho National Laboratory). Key alloys: Inconel 718, GRCop-84, IN625, 17-4 SS, and copper alloys.

Area 4 — Additive Manufacturing of Graded and Tailored Alloys for Aerospace

Co-led with the Air Force Research Laboratory, developing functionally graded and compositionally tailored alloys for sustainment and new aerospace applications.

Area 5 — Additive Manufacturing of Ceramics & Ceramic Matrix Composites

Direct ink writing and post-processing of SiC, ZrC, Si₃N₄, and SiC–SiC CMCs for hypersonic vehicles, aircraft engine noise reduction, nuclear thermal propulsion, and space applications. Sponsors include NASA, NETL, Army SBIR, and GOMACTech partners.

Area 6 — Bio-Inspired Manufacturing of Metal–Graphene Composites (NSF CAREER)

NSF CAREER project (2020–2025) developing high-strength, high-toughness metal–graphene composites inspired by hierarchical natural structures.

Area 7 — Additive Manufacturing of Continuous Carbon Fiber Reinforced Composites

Laser-assisted and ultrasonic-assisted laminated object manufacturing, graphene-enhanced interlayer bonding, and mechanical/ultrasonic finishing of CFRP. The Composite Structures review (Parandoush & Lin, 2017) is the #1 most-downloaded article in that journal since 2020, and U.S. Patent No. 11,254,048 (2022) is licensed to HydroGraph.

Area 8 — Sustainable & Bio-Based Manufacturing

Decentralized pelletization of agricultural and forestry residues for bioenergy (USDA-NIFA Sun Grant), lignin-based wearable triboelectric nanogenerators for health monitoring (USDA-NIFA), and bio-inspired nanocellulose aerogel acoustic materials.
